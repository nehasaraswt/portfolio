The Context

Throughout my career, I've been fortunate to design and build at par with US peers — owning products, driving strategy, and working as a full design partner rather than a support function. I didn't experience the ceiling many Indian designers ran into. But I could see it clearly beyond my own teams.

The Indian design community was apprehensive of US-based companies — and not without reason. The concern was that working for a global tech firm would mean replicating or supporting what US teams had already decided. Not owning a product. Not making the consequential calls. The fear was real: talented designers choosing not to join, or joining and quietly looking for a way out, because they sensed the work wouldn't be theirs.

Across three organisations — 247.ai, Walmart, and Intuit — my job was two-sided. I had to ensure that great talent wanted to come work with us. And I had to ensure that once they did, they got to work on great products — not derivative work, not support work, but real design accountability on real products. Both sides mattered equally. One without the other doesn't hold.

The Approach

Seed teams, don't staff positions

Rather than backfilling headcount into existing structures, I focused on seeding product teams with design capability — pairing designers with engineers and PMs from day one, establishing design presence in the room where decisions were made, not downstream of them.

Build career paths, not just teams

Designers in India needed to see where they could go without leaving the country. I worked to create growth tracks, mentorship structures, and visibility with global leadership — so that advancement didn't require relocation. Several designers I worked with moved into leadership roles at Intuit India and beyond.

Establish culture before process

Process without culture collapses the moment you step back. I focused on design culture first — craft standards, critique rituals, shared vocabulary, and a sense of ownership over product outcomes. The process followed naturally from that foundation.

Create community infrastructure

Individual teams can't carry a discipline forward on their own. I convened and led #D4 — Intuit India's design conference — built around the philosophy of "Design for Delight." It gave designers across Intuit India a shared stage, a shared language, and a reason to think of themselves as part of something bigger than a single product team.

#D4 became more than an event. Designers submitted talks, debated ideas, and came away with a sense that the work they were doing in India was the work — not a reduced version of it. It established a standard for what design culture in India could look like, and gave designers a stage to grow into, not just occupy.

The Scale of the Work

Across Intuit, Walmart, and 247.ai, I grew design organisations in India from 17 to 62 designers. That number understates what actually happened: alongside headcount growth, I was building the infrastructure around which design could compound — hiring practices, onboarding models, design systems, mentorship programmes, and relationships with global product leadership.

At Intuit specifically, the community grew from a seeded group into a 50+ designer presence across product teams. This work ran concurrently with leading QB FP&A, QuickBooks Desktop, and QuickBooks Online — not as a separate initiative, but woven into how I operated every day.

Legacy

Talent pipelines, career paths, and design culture that outlasted my tenure at each organisation

  • Several designers I mentored moved into mid-level and leadership roles at Intuit and other organisations
  • Some went on to start their own design studios in India
  • Contributed to Intuit recognising India as a design capability centre — not just an engineering resource
  • Helped establish design as a visible, viable career path in India's enterprise tech sector
  • Built models for cross-geography design leadership that influenced how distributed teams operated

What Made It Notable

  • Org-building as a design problem — Growing a design capability requires the same systems thinking as product design: understanding users (designers), mapping the journey (career path), removing friction (barriers to advancement), and building for scale.
  • Community as infrastructure — #D4 wasn't a nice-to-have. It was how design culture became self-sustaining rather than dependent on any single leader.
  • Designed for longevity — The measure of this work isn't what existed while I was there. It's what continued after I left.
