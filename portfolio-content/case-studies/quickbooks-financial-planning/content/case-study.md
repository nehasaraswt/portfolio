# Building a 0→1 Financial Planning Product While Establishing Design Talent in India
## QuickBooks Online — FP&A Design Leadership & Execution

### The Challenge

QuickBooks Online had established itself as the bookkeeping backbone for millions of small and mid-market businesses (SMBs). But for mid-market companies, the product had a critical gap: while it excelled at **recording** financial data, it offered no integrated tools for **planning** and **forecasting**.

These customers were forced into fragmented workflows—using Excel, disconnected planning tools, and external platforms to answer forward-looking questions:
- *What will my cash flow look like next quarter?*
- *How should I adjust inventory based on demand forecasts?*
- *Which business scenarios are most profitable?*

This wasn't a UX problem. It was a **product strategy problem**. And it was costing Intuit millions in mid-market share, since these customers outgrew QuickBooks or maintained subscriptions to three different tools simultaneously.

**My mandate**: Design and lead the creation of a new financial planning and analysis product from inception through general availability, while building a design team and establishing India as a design capability center for Intuit.

### The Approach

Rather than jump into design, I started with **strategic discovery**. But critically, I did this while **simultaneously building a design team** in India—a move that was unconventional but strategic.

#### Phase 1: Discovery + Team Building (Months 1-4)

I conducted discovery across three stakeholder groups while recruiting India-based designers:

**Discovery Work** (I led this personally):
- Interviewed 40+ mid-market finance teams to understand planning workflows and pain points
- Mapped competitive landscape (Adaptive Insights, Anaplan, Prophix)
- Workshopped with Intuit leadership on product strategy and success criteria

**Simultaneous: Building the Team**
- Hired 8 designers in India (freshly graduated, high potential)
- Recruited 2 senior designers from US to lead India operations
- Built a cross-functional core: 5 designers, 8 engineers, 6 product/analytics

**Why India? Why Now?**
This decision was deliberate and strategic:
- Intuit was investing in building design capability outside the US
- India has exceptional design talent but limited opportunities in product design
- I saw an opportunity to **build something exceptional while establishing India as a design center**

I personally mentored the 8 junior designers through their first projects. This wasn't delegated—I invested significant time in making sure they understood the design philosophy and could execute at the level the product required.

![Team Structure: 14-Person Design Team](../../images/process/process-team-structure.png)

#### Phase 2: Strategic Positioning (Months 2-4)

I made three pivotal strategic **design decisions** (personally):

**Decision 1: Reporting vs. Planning Focus**
- ❌ **Not**: Extend QuickBooks with more advanced financial reports
- ✅ **Instead**: Build a planning-first product centered on scenario modeling and forecasting

*My design thinking*: Reporting was table stakes. Every competitor had reporting. The defensible moat was helping SMBs **plan proactively**—something no existing QB feature did well. I designed the entire experience around this principle.

**Decision 2: Simplify Complexity Through Design**
- ❌ **Not**: Require finance experts to build models
- ✅ **Instead**: Embed AI to democratize planning through intuitive interfaces

*My execution*: I personally designed:
- The **Forecast Builder** interface (guided, step-by-step, non-threatening)
- The **Scenario Comparison** experience (side-by-side, focused on impact)
- The **Insights Dashboard** (surfacing AI recommendations naturally)

Each design was tested with real customers and iterated based on feedback. I spent weeks refining the forecast builder's language and flow—details matter when you're making complex financial tasks approachable.

**Decision 3: Product Packaging & Business Model**
- ❌ **Not**: Build another feature within QB Online
- ✅ **Instead**: Position as a **new, premium SKU** within the Intuit ecosystem

*Why this mattered*: This decision rippled across the entire organization. It wasn't just a design decision—it influenced product roadmaps, sales strategy, and enterprise tier positioning. I advocated for this internally, contributing to what became the **Intuit Enterprise Suite**.

#### Phase 3: Hands-On Design Execution (Months 4-20)

**This is where being hands-on was critical.**

I personally designed the core product experiences:

**1. Forecast Builder** (I designed this)
- Guided interface where non-experts could build financial forecasts
- Historical data selection → forecast method choice → AI-powered recommendations
- I personally iterated on the language, visual hierarchy, and interaction model 20+ times based on user testing

**2. Scenario Modeling** (I designed this)
- Users could create "what-if" scenarios and compare outcomes side-by-side
- I designed the comparison interface to show cascading financial impact
- Critical design challenge: make the math visible without overwhelming the user

**3. Financial Insights Dashboard** (I designed this)
- AI-driven summary of key trends, anomalies, and risks
- I designed this to surface the most important information automatically
- Design principle: reduce cognitive load (finance teams spend 60% gathering data)

**4. Approval Workflows** (I designed this)
- Multi-level approval processes for forecasts and budgets
- I designed this to make governance clear without adding friction
- Who approves? What are they deciding? Timeline?

**5. Design System Foundation** (I established this)
- Created the component library, patterns, and guidelines
- Trained the 8 junior designers to use and extend the system
- This system became the foundation for their entire work

### The Hands-On Design Work

Here's what the actual design process looked like:

#### Forecast Builder (My Design)
**Problem**: Non-experts need to build financial forecasts without being intimidated.

**My Design Approach**:
1. **Guided Experience**: Step-by-step wizard (select data → choose method → review AI insights)
2. **Familiar Mental Models**: Used "templates" based on common forecast scenarios
3. **Transparency**: Showed underlying assumptions and let users override them
4. **AI as Assistant**: Presented AI recommendations as "suggestions to consider," not mandates

**Design Iteration**: I iterated on this interface 20+ times based on user testing. Key learnings:
- Users wanted to see *why* the AI recommended something
- Complex terminology ("exponential smoothing") scared people; "trend-based" was better
- Showing confidence levels on forecasts was critical for trust

![Forecast Builder Interface](../../images/solution/solution-forecast-builder.png)

#### Scenario Comparison (My Design)
**Problem**: How do you help users understand financial impact of "what-if" decisions?

**My Design Solution**:
- Side-by-side scenario comparison (Scenario A vs. Scenario B)
- Key metrics highlighted (revenue, margin, cash position)
- Visual indicators showing divergence between scenarios
- Drill-down capability to understand where changes came from

**Critical Design Challenge**: Users tried to optimize for the *wrong* metrics. I designed progressive disclosure so the interface started with macro-level metrics (cash position, profitability) before diving into details.

#### Insights Dashboard (My Design)
**Problem**: Finance team members spend 60% of their time *gathering* data. Design should reduce this.

**My Design Solution**:
- AI-surfaced the most important metrics automatically
- Anomaly detection highlighted unusual patterns
- Trend forecasting showed what's likely to happen next
- All tied back to actionable insights ("cash position declining—recommend adjusting expenses")

**Design Philosophy**: Make intelligence visible. The underlying statistical models were sophisticated; the interface was approachable.

### Key Decisions & Why

**Decision 1: Be Hands-On While Building a Team**
- **Alternative**: Hire an experienced design leader to execute, I focus on strategy
- **Why I chose hands-on**: The market and design challenges were novel. I needed to personally understand the problem space to make the right strategic choices. Plus, I wanted the team to learn by watching how I approached design problems.
- **Outcome**: The team learned design thinking by osmosis. They understood *why* we made certain decisions, not just *what* to build.

**Decision 2: Build Design Capability in India**
- **Alternative**: Keep all design in the US where design was already established
- **Why I chose India**: Strategic investment in emerging markets + opportunity to tap into India's talent + chance to establish design as a career path in a market that needed it
- **Risk**: Junior designers would need mentorship; slower initial execution
- **Outcome**: Built a sustainable design capability that lasted beyond this project. Many of those junior designers became design leaders.

**Decision 3: Focus on Forecast Builder UX**
- **Alternative**: Treat it as a transactional interface, focus on engineering
- **Why I chose design-first**: If the forecast builder was intimidating, the whole product failed. This had to be the *most* thoughtfully designed part of the product.
- **Outcome**: Users said the forecast builder was the primary reason they chose our product over competitors.

**Decision 4: Introduce AI Early**
- **Alternative**: Launch with traditional planning tools, add AI later
- **Why I chose to include AI from the start**: Customer research showed SMBs didn't have finance expertise. AI was the leverage point to make sophisticated planning accessible.
- **Outcome**: AI became a major competitive advantage. Intuit continued expanding AI across the suite.

### Lessons Learned

**1. You Can Lead AND Execute**
The pressure was to choose: "Are you a designer or a leader?" I chose both. I spent 40% of my time doing hands-on design, 60% leading the team. This hybrid approach worked because:
- The team learned by watching design in action
- I understood the design challenges deeply enough to make strategic calls
- I could mentor through specific design problems, not just abstractly

**2. Mentoring Junior Designers is High-Leverage**
The 8 junior designers I hired had potential but no product experience. Investing time in mentoring them:
- Paid dividends as they became productive faster
- Built team culture around learning
- Some of those junior designers are now mid-level leaders

**3. Geography Matters Less Than You Think**
Working with a distributed team (US + India) could have been a bottleneck. Instead:
- I established clear design processes and critiques
- Async feedback loops worked well
- Time zone difference meant continuous iteration

**4. Design Systems Are Mentorship Tools**
The design system I created wasn't just about consistency. It was:
- A teaching tool (here's how we solve this problem)
- A quality gate (maintains standards even as team grows)
- A culture artifact (here's how we think about design)

**5. Strategic Product Design > UI Design**
The most impactful work I did was **positioning** the product (planning vs. reporting) and **packaging** it (new SKU vs. feature). The UI was well-executed, but those strategic decisions had 10x more impact.

**What I'd Do Differently**:
- **Earlier executive alignment**: Getting buy-in on the new SKU strategy earlier would have reduced political friction
- **More aggressive hiring**: We grew the India team to 14 eventually, but could have started with 10 from day 1
- **More intentional India investment**: This project accidentally became a flagship for India capability; doing it intentionally from the start would have amplified the impact

## Takeaways for Design Leadership

This project demonstrated that **the best design leaders**:

1. **Get Their Hands Dirty**: Don't delegate the hard design work. Do the design yourself, then lead others to do it better.
2. **Make Strategic Choices**: Positioning and packaging matter more than pixel perfection.
3. **Build Capability, Not Just Products**: Leave the organization more capable than you found it.
4. **Lead With Design**: Show the team how you approach problems, then help them develop their own approach.
5. **Measure Impact**: Design success isn't just user satisfaction—it's business impact (adoption, revenue) and team growth.
