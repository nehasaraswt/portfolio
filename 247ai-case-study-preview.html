The Challenge

[247].ai's conversational AI platform served Fortune 500 enterprises — automating customer interactions across voice, digital, and chat. But the platform had been assembled through acquisitions, and it showed. Products that handled different parts of the customer journey didn't speak to each other. There was no shared design language, no consistent interaction model, and no unified picture of how AI and human agents handed off to each other.

The result: fragmented customer experiences, siloed product teams, and a platform that couldn't be sold — or operated — as a platform.

The Opportunity

The field didn't yet have language for what we were building. AI-human handoffs, agent-assisted resolution, multimodal engagement — we were designing these in practice while the industry was still theorising about them. That meant starting from first principles: who does this system actually serve, what does a good handoff feel like, and what does a design system look like when the product it governs is half-human, half-machine?

I ran follow-the-user studies, mapped how the platform's fragmented pieces were being used versus how they were supposed to connect, and benchmarked across the industry to understand where conversational AI was heading.

Key Decisions

Design system, built from scratch

There was no shared visual or interaction language across the platform. I built one — components, patterns, motion, and governance — across all product surfaces. It became the standard the entire organisation operated on, and the mechanism through which previously isolated teams could finally work as one platform.

AI-human handoff patterns, before the industry named them

The most critical design problem was the seam between AI and human agents: when does the bot hand off, what does the agent inherit, and how does the customer experience the transition? I designed those patterns across voice, digital, and chat. What we built here predates what the industry now calls "agentic UX."

Design sprints as the unification mechanism

Fragmentation was partly a visibility problem — teams couldn't see how their products connected to the larger system. I ran all organisation-wide design sprints, surfacing those connections and moving decisions that had been stalled for years. By end of 2019, most systems were standardised and working together.

The Work

The platform was redefined as a Customer Engagement Cloud and a Customer Acquisition Cloud with a shared reporting and insight layer — giving the product an architecture it had never had.

  • Built the design system from scratch — visual language, interaction patterns, motion, and governance across the entire product portfolio
  • Designed AI-human handoff patterns — for voice, digital, and chat surfaces; defining what agents inherit at handoff and how customers experience the transition
  • Ran all organisation-wide design sprints — surfacing cross-product connections, aligning isolated teams, and unblocking stalled decisions
  • Unified acquired products under a coherent platform architecture — from loose collection of tools to a managed, modular self-serve platform
