The Context

[247].ai was building something that didn't yet have a category name. The platform helped Fortune 500 enterprises — Cisco, Hilton, Target, Nike Football — automate customer conversations across voice, digital, and chat, with human agents stepping in where the AI handed off. I joined as Director of Design in 2016 to lead design for the platform and its workforce management layer.

The platform had grown through acquisitions, which meant I was working across a collection of capable products that had their own design languages and their own teams. My job was to make them into one coherent thing — a Customer Engagement Cloud and Customer Acquisition Cloud with a shared intelligence layer — and to design the experiences that made it work as a whole.

On the WFM side, the opportunity was clear: contact centre agents are fast, skilled people doing high-stakes work, and most of the tools they used every day were designed for the people who procured them — not the people who lived in them. I wanted to change that.

The Process

Whiteboard session mapping WFM platform flows and screen architecture — the information architecture that became the agent portal

I started by spending time with the people the platform was built to serve: agents on the floor, supervisors managing queues in real time, workforce planners building schedules weeks out. Each role had a completely different relationship with time and information. I also benchmarked across the six dominant WFM vendors — Verint, NICE/IEX, Genesys, Aspect, Calabrio, Invision — to understand what the field was doing and where the real design opportunity sat.

IA session mapping Cisco's product hierarchy — Chat and OPS products, their sub-channels, and how they connected to the customer journey across voice, IVR, and digital

The IA work shown above was real client work: mapping how Cisco's products — Chat, OPS, Routex, Webex, and IVR — related to each other across the customer journey. That map became the basis for how we designed the connections between them.

Key Decisions

WFM designed for agents, not administrators

Agents using this platform worked fast, in noisy environments, moving between tasks all day. I designed the agent portal around what they actually needed during a shift: a clear schedule, an easy way to request swaps, real-time queue status they could act on. Simplicity over configuration — because a tool that gets used is more valuable than a tool that can theoretically do everything.

The handoff as the most important moment in every interaction

When AIVA — the AI agent — passes a conversation to a human, that transition matters. The human agent needs to know what just happened. The customer needs to feel continuity. I designed that moment carefully across voice, chat, and digital: customer context surfaced at handoff, intent carried across, conversation history visible and ready. In 2016 nobody had worked this out at scale. The patterns we built here are what people now call agentic UX.

Design system as the shared language

I built the design system from scratch — components, patterns, motion, governance. The practical value was visual consistency. The deeper value was that shared components created shared decisions: teams that had been working independently started to see how their products connected, and making something together became possible. By end of 2019, the platform was working as one.

The Work — AIVA Handoff Console

AIVA was [247].ai's conversational AI — handling the first layer of every customer interaction before a human agent took over. The handoff between them is the critical moment: where the platform either feels continuous or falls apart. I designed the agent-facing console that made that moment work.

When the AI hands off, the agent is ready. Customer intent, conversation history, and suggested first responses are surfaced immediately — so the agent steps into the conversation informed, picks up from where AIVA left off, and the customer experiences it as one interaction, not two.

Platform Architecture — Customer Engagement Cloud

The platform was eventually defined and architected as two clouds — a Customer Engagement Cloud and a Customer Acquisition Cloud — with a shared reporting and insight layer serving both. Getting to that architecture required making the system's parts visible to each other for the first time: NL Tools, Chat Miner, Bot Builder, Prediction Engine, and Unified Admin Console on the intelligence layer; WFM, Reporting, Billing, and Optimisation on the enterprise operations layer; and the Agent Console on the engagement layer.

Platform architecture — the three-legged model: Visitor (customer-facing AI), Agents (console, engagement, WFM), and Enterprise (optimisation, billing, reporting). The shared intelligence layer — Prediction Engine, NL Tools, Bot Builder — served all three.

The Design System

There was no shared visual language across the platform when I joined. Components, colour, motion, and interaction patterns had evolved independently across teams. I built the design system from scratch — defining the colour architecture across eight semantic palettes (Orange, Magenta, Purple, Lilac, Blue, Green, Red, Neutral), interaction tokens, component specifications, and the governance model that ensured teams adopted rather than worked around it.

[24]7.ai design system colour architecture — eight semantic palettes built for a platform that ran voice, digital, and chat interfaces simultaneously. The design system was crowd-sourced across teams and became the operating standard across all product surfaces.

What Made It Notable

  • First-principles design for a new category — AI-human handoffs, multimodal engagement, agentic UX: in 2016 these were problems we were solving in production, for real clients, with no established patterns to borrow from. The interaction principles we derived here became the foundation for how the industry eventually approached this work.
  • Designing for the people doing the work — the WFM agent portal was built around one question: does this work for someone on the floor, mid-shift, with no time to think about it? That discipline — starting with the person with the least time, not the most — shaped every decision in the system.
  • Design system as the connective tissue — shared components meant shared decisions. The design system gave teams across the organisation a common language, and that common language is what made the platform cohere. Unification happened through design, not through a technical rewrite.
  • The handoff as the centre of the experience — treating the AI-to-human transition as the most important moment in every interaction changed how the whole console was conceived. That perspective, which now feels obvious, had to be argued for and designed from scratch in 2017.
