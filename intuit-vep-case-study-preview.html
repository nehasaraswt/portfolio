Designing the human-AI collaboration layer for Intuit's Virtual Expert Platform — autonomous triage and resolution, natural language customer conversations, and enterprise appointment scheduling

Virtual Expert Platform

The Context

Intuit's Virtual Expert Platform connected small businesses with financial experts — but the experience was largely manual, human-coordinated, and fragmented across touchpoints. I led design for three of VEP's most critical workstreams: autonomous operations (AI-driven triage and resolution), conversations (natural language interface between customers and expert systems), and appointments (enterprise scheduling, launched to 10K customers at Intuit Enterprise Suite).

This was not a v2 project. The platform needed to be rebuilt around what AI could now do — with no existing industry patterns for how to design human-AI handoffs at this scale.

The Opportunity

The core design problem was that AI and humans needed to share the platform — not take turns. Where does AI act autonomously? Where does it escalate? How does that transition feel trustworthy rather than abrupt? Getting that right across three distinct surfaces — operations, conversations, and appointments — was the challenge.

Appointments was the clearest entry point: a concrete, high-frequency task that worked across business sizes. At the July 2025 Intuit Enterprise Suite launch, it went live to 10K enterprise customers — enabling businesses to schedule directly with their customers, end-to-end through the platform.

Key Decisions

Autonomous-first, not assisted-first

The default state of the platform was AI acting — not AI assisting. That required inverting typical UX assumptions: instead of showing the AI helping, the question became how a human steps in when AI can't. Every interface decision across all three workstreams was anchored to that inversion.

Human-AI handoffs as a first-class design problem

Seamless handoffs require legibility on both sides: the human needs to know what the AI has done, and the customer needs to trust the transition. I defined the interaction principles governing when AI acts autonomously, when it escalates to human judgment, and how decisions are explained and overridden — before the industry had established patterns for this.

Trust built into the interface, not assumed from the AI

Users would only accept AI-driven resolution if they trusted what the AI was doing. That trust couldn't be asserted — it had to be designed. Transparency about AI actions, clear override paths, and legible escalation logic were core requirements across all three workstreams, not edge cases.

The Work

  • Autonomous operations — designed AI-driven triage, resolution, and escalation workflows; replaced manual human-in-the-loop coordination with low-touch agentic models
  • Conversations — designed the natural language interface layer: how customers interact with expert systems before a human agent is ever involved, including fallback and handoff patterns
  • Appointments — launched enterprise scheduling to 10K customers at Intuit Enterprise Suite; B2C scheduling integrated natively into the QuickBooks ecosystem
  • Interaction principles — established the design language for human-AI collaboration on VEP: when AI acts, when it escalates, how decisions surface and can be overridden

The Results

+45%

Live services revenue growth — connecting customers with experts at scale through the platform

~40%

Cost reduction through automation and elimination of manual coordination

10K

Enterprise customers launched with appointment capabilities at Intuit Enterprise Suite

What Made It Notable

  • Designing before patterns existed — interaction principles for autonomous AI in enterprise had to be created, not borrowed. The industry was still theorising; we were shipping.
  • Coherence across three distinct surfaces — autonomous operations, conversational interfaces, and scheduling are different UX domains with different users and rhythms. Keeping them coherent while each had its own launch cadence was the real design challenge.
  • Trust as a design constraint, not a feature — building user trust into the interface itself — through transparency, override paths, and legible AI decisions — was the foundational requirement for any of the automation gains to be realised.
