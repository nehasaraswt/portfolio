Reimagining Intuit's Virtual Expert Platform as an autonomous, AI-first operation — from expert scheduling and training to customer conversations and enterprise appointments

The Context

Intuit's Virtual Expert Platform operated at a scale most platforms never reach — 25 million customer contacts per year, 50,000 experts, 535 million live meeting minutes, 95% SLA across every touchpoint. But despite that scale, the platform ran largely on manual coordination: expert schedules built by hand, training disconnected from call performance, customer routing done without system intelligence, and appointment booking fragmented across products.

In 2023, the mandate changed. Expert Operations needed to become an AI-native, autonomous platform — not because it was technically possible, but because operating at this scale with manual systems was a ceiling that the business had already hit. I led design across the four workstreams that made that shift real: Shift Builder, Appointments, Autonomous Operations, and the Conversations platform underpinning all of them.

The Framework: Human-Agent Tandem

Before designing any surface, I needed a model for what was being designed. The existing framing — AI assisting humans — was wrong for what we were building. At this scale, the platform couldn't be structured around humans doing the work with AI helping. It needed to be structured around AI acting, with humans available for what AI can't handle.

The framework I developed to ground the team was Human-Agent Tandem: humans and AI agents as a set of deployable skills, matched in real time against the specific intent, context, and customer need of each interaction. No single human is ever a 100% match for a unique customer situation — there will always be a gap. That gap, if left unaddressed, shows up as quality in the short term and performance in the long term.

The core insight

AI supplement agents should fill the gap just-in-time and just-enough — stepping in to fill a conversation gap, support the expert mid-call, take over a segment of the interaction, or train the expert on the side while it handles the moment. Over time, as known intents become fully automated, humans stay agile for the unknown unknowns: the novel, unpredictable cases where human judgment is irreplaceable.

This framework governed interaction principles across every workstream: when AI acts autonomously, when it escalates, how handoffs are signalled, and how trust is built into the interface rather than asserted by it.

Key Decisions

Autonomous-first, not assisted-first

The default state of every surface was AI acting — not AI helping. That required inverting standard UX assumptions: instead of designing for an AI that surfaces options, I designed for an AI that makes decisions, with humans stepping in only when the AI can't proceed. That inversion changed everything from information architecture to escalation design to how error states were framed.

Trust built into the interface, not assumed from the AI

Experts and customers would only accept AI-driven decisions if they understood what the AI had done and could override it clearly. Transparency about AI actions, legible escalation logic, and explicit override paths were core requirements — not edge cases. Designing that legibility at scale, across five surfaces with different users and rhythms, was the foundational design challenge of the platform.

Closed-loop design: performance feeds training feeds performance

The platform needed to learn from itself. I designed the connection between post-call performance data and expert training: Flight Simulator, an AI-driven personalised training system, generated targeted practice scenarios based on each expert's actual call patterns and gaps — not generic content. That loop — act, measure, train, improve — was the structural shift from a static operation to a self-improving one.

The Work

Shift Builder — Redesigning Expert Scheduling

Before Shift Builder, expert managers spent an average of 54 minutes per week building schedules manually — matching 50,000 experts to demand forecasts, availability patterns, and skill requirements by hand. The system couldn't learn from patterns, couldn't adapt to intraday demand shifts, and couldn't scale.

Shift Builder replaced that entirely with AI-optimised weekly scheduling: the system ingests historical demand, expert availability and performance data, and outputs schedules in minutes. Managers validate rather than build. The result was an 85% reduction in scheduling time — from 54 minutes to 8 — and 13,500 expert work hours recovered across the platform each week that had previously been spent in scheduling overhead.

Appointments — Enterprise Scheduling at Launch

Appointment booking was the clearest 0→1 workstream: a concrete, high-frequency task that required designing an end-to-end scheduling experience from scratch, integrated natively into the QuickBooks ecosystem. The challenge wasn't just scheduling — it was designing for trust: customers needed to be confident they'd reached the right expert, at the right time, with context already established.

At the July 2025 Intuit Enterprise Suite launch, Appointments went live to 10,000 enterprise customers. The design measurably shifted behaviour: reschedule-within-5-minutes rate dropped from 28% to 6% (a signal of booking confidence), View-to-Book ratio improved from 28% to 44% for Full Service customers, and Time to Convert decreased from 1 minute 4 seconds to 48 seconds.

Autonomous Operations — AI-Driven Triage and Resolution

Autonomous Ops was the hardest workstream: designing interfaces for AI that acts rather than assists, across triage, routing, resolution, and escalation. The deliverables included the AI agent dashboard for operations managers, the real-time routing and matching interface, the escalation UI that surfaces AI reasoning to human reviewers, and the override mechanisms that kept humans accountable without making autonomy fragile. In FY25, VEP reached 120 ITPY (interactions per expert per year) connections — a platform connectivity milestone that required every operations surface to function as a coherent system rather than individual tools.

Conversations — Natural Language Platform Layer

The Conversations workstream designed the natural language interface that customers use before a human expert is ever involved: intent capture, context building, smart routing, and handoff from AI to human with full context preserved. The design challenge was preventing both under-escalation (AI trying to resolve what it shouldn't) and over-escalation (unnecessary handoffs that erode trust in the AI). Fallback logic, handoff framing, and context continuity were first-class design requirements — not edge cases to handle later.

What Made It Notable

  • A framework before a feature — Human-Agent Tandem gave the team a principled model for what human-AI collaboration should actually look like at this scale, before a single screen was designed. That grounding kept five workstreams coherent across different launch cadences and different user types.
  • Designing before patterns existed — there were no established interaction models for autonomous operations in enterprise at this scale. The principles for when AI acts, when it escalates, how decisions are surfaced and overridden — all of that had to be invented, not borrowed.
  • Closed-loop intelligence — the platform learned from itself. Connecting post-call performance data to personalised training (Flight Simulator) turned the platform from a static operation into one that improves with use. That design decision had long-term structural value beyond any individual metric.
  • Five distinct surfaces, one coherent system — scheduling, training, routing, conversations, and appointments are different UX domains with different users, rhythms, and mental models. Making them feel like a single platform — while each had its own launch deadline and stakeholder group — was the real design leadership challenge.
